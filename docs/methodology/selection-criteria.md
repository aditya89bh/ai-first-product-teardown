# Product Selection and Prioritisation Criteria

Many products are [eligible](inclusion-criteria.md); we cannot tear all of them down at
once. This document defines how we choose *which eligible products to study first*.
Prioritisation is a transparent judgement, not a ranking of product quality, and it is
recorded so that the queue can be challenged.

## Prioritisation factors

Each eligible candidate is assessed on the following factors. Factors are considered
together as a qualitative judgement; they are **not** summed into a single score, to avoid
the false precision the library rejects (see
[scoring false precision](../../frameworks/scoring-false-precision.md)).

1. **Learning value.** How much does studying this product teach about how AI-first products
   work in general? Products that exemplify or challenge a pattern rank higher.
2. **Audience relevance.** How directly does it serve founders, product leaders, designers,
   researchers or students?
3. **Evidential tractability.** How readily can we gather graded evidence? A product we can
   observe first-hand and corroborate ranks higher than one we can only read about.
4. **Coverage and contrast.** Does it fill a gap in the library, or provide a useful
   contrast to products already covered? Diversity of approaches is valuable.
5. **Durability.** Is it stable enough that a teardown will remain useful for a reasonable
   window, or so volatile that findings decay immediately?
6. **Timeliness.** Is there a specific reason this product is worth studying now (a notable
   shift in approach, adoption, or discourse) grounded in evidence rather than hype?

## How the factors combine

- We reason about the whole profile, not a weighted average. A product exceptional on
  learning value can be prioritised despite weaker tractability, provided we can still meet
  the evidence bar.
- **Evidential tractability is a floor, not just a factor.** If we cannot meet the
  [evidence hierarchy](evidence-hierarchy.md) requirements, the product waits regardless of
  how interesting it is.
- Ties are broken toward **coverage and contrast**, because a balanced library teaches more
  than a cluster of near-identical teardowns.

## Anti-patterns we avoid

- **Hype-chasing.** Popularity or media volume is not, by itself, a reason to prioritise.
  Timeliness must be justified by an evidenced shift, not by attention.
- **Vendor influence.** Requests, sponsorships or relationships never move a product up the
  queue; if anything they trigger conflict-of-interest handling.
- **Recency bias.** Newest is not automatically most important; a stable, widely used
  product may teach more than a fresh launch.

## Recording and revisiting

- The active queue and the rationale for prioritising each item are kept in the open so
  contributors can propose reordering with evidence.
- Priorities are revisited each phase, informed by gaps that comparisons and syntheses
  reveal.
