# Researcher Bias and Conflict-of-Interest Rules

Rigorous method does not eliminate bias; it constrains it. This document defines the biases
most likely to distort a teardown, the conflicts of interest that must be declared, and the
controls that keep both from silently shaping conclusions.

## Conflicts of interest

A conflict of interest exists when a contributor has an interest that could reasonably be
seen to influence their analysis of a product. Examples:

- Employment, contracting, advisory, or ownership relationship with the product's maker or a
  direct competitor.
- Financial position that would benefit from a favourable or unfavourable teardown.
- Personal relationships with people who built or fund the product.
- Prior public advocacy for or against the product that would be embarrassing to contradict.

### Declaration and handling

1. **Declare before contributing.** Any contributor to a teardown declares relevant
   conflicts in the teardown's metadata before work begins.
2. **Recuse where influence is material.** For a serious conflict, the contributor does not
   author or approve that teardown; they may still supply clearly-labelled evidence.
3. **Disclose to readers.** Managed conflicts that remain relevant are disclosed in the
   published teardown so readers can weigh them.
4. **Unmanageable conflicts exclude the product** from that contributor's work, per the
   [inclusion criteria](../methodology/inclusion-criteria.md).

## Cognitive biases we actively counter

- **Confirmation bias** — seeking evidence that fits a prior view. Counter: require
  disconfirming search; a teardown must record evidence that *cuts against* its own
  conclusions where such evidence exists.
- **Demo bias** — over-weighting a single impressive or disappointing interaction. Counter:
  [triangulation](triangulation.md) and repeated trials before any behavioural fact.
- **Recency bias** — treating the newest product or observation as most important. Counter:
  the [recency policy](../methodology/recency-policy.md) dates everything and the
  [selection criteria](../methodology/selection-criteria.md) resist hype-chasing.
- **Anchoring on the vendor narrative** — accepting stated intent as observed behaviour.
  Counter: separate E2 (primary/stated) from E1 (observed) and never let one stand in for the
  other.
- **Halo / horns effects** — letting a strong impression on one dimension colour the others.
  Counter: the six dimensions are analysed on their own evidence, not as a single verdict.

## Structural controls

- **Independent review.** Verification is done by someone other than the author (see
  [claim verification](claim-verification.md)).
- **Classification discipline.** Labelling every claim fact/observation/inference/hypothesis
  makes bias visible: an inflated inference stands out.
- **Disconfirmation requirement.** Reviewers explicitly ask, "what evidence would change this
  conclusion, and did the author look for it?"
- **No favours in prioritisation.** Vendor requests and relationships never move a product up
  the queue (see [selection criteria](../methodology/selection-criteria.md)).

## Principle

We assume every researcher, including ourselves, is biased. The defence is not claimed
neutrality but *visible method*: declared conflicts, separated claim types, triangulated
evidence, and independent review. A teardown that hides its biases is less trustworthy than
one that declares and controls them.
