# Archival and Deprecation Policy

Some teardowns describe products that change beyond recognition, get discontinued, or are
superseded by a newer version. Rather than deleting this work, the library **archives** it,
preserving its research value while making clear it no longer describes a current product.
This policy defines when and how teardowns are deprecated and archived.

## Definitions

- **Active** — describes a current unit of analysis within its recency window; the default
  state.
- **Stale** — still describes the current product, but its evidence has aged past the
  comfortable window and it awaits re-review (see [recency](../methodology/recency-policy.md)).
- **Deprecated** — no longer describes the current product (the product changed materially,
  was discontinued, or the teardown was superseded), but is retained as a historical record.
- **Withdrawn** — removed from the library as a source of findings because it was found
  unsound; the withdrawal and its reason remain visible.

## When a teardown is deprecated

A teardown is deprecated when any of the following holds:

1. The product changed enough to invalidate its [unit of analysis](../methodology/unit-of-analysis.md)
   and a new MAJOR version supersedes it (see [versioning](versioning.md)).
2. The product is discontinued or no longer reachable for study.
3. A newer teardown covers the same unit more accurately and the old one would mislead if
   presented as current.

## How archival works

- **Retain, don't delete.** Deprecated teardowns stay in the repository, moved or marked as
  archived, with their evidence records intact. Deleting them would destroy the historical
  and comparative record.
- **Label prominently.** An archived teardown carries a clear banner: its status, the date it
  was deprecated, why, and a pointer to any superseding teardown.
- **Freeze the content.** Archived teardowns are not edited to appear current; they are
  preserved as the dated snapshot they always were.
- **Keep it discoverable but not misleading.** Archived work remains searchable for
  historical and comparative use, but is excluded from anything that implies current findings.

## Withdrawal vs deprecation

- **Deprecation** says: *this was accurate for its time and product state; that state has
  passed.* The findings were sound.
- **Withdrawal** says: *this was unsound and should not be relied on.* Used when a teardown
  fails verification after publication and cannot be corrected.
- Both preserve a visible record; neither erases history. The distinction protects readers
  from mistaking a superseded-but-sound teardown for a discredited one.

## Historical and comparative value

Archived teardowns are not dead weight. They enable:

- **Longitudinal analysis** — how a product's approach to a dimension changed over time.
- **Pattern discovery** — recurring trajectories across products and eras.
- **Honesty about our own track record** — showing what we concluded and when.

## Principle

The library keeps its history. Products come and go; the record of how we studied them, and
what was true when, is itself a research asset worth preserving.
