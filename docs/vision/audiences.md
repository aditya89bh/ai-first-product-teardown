# Target Audiences and Use Cases

The library serves five primary audiences. Each has different questions, different tolerance
for detail, and a different definition of "useful". The method is designed so that a single
teardown can serve all five without diluting rigour: humans read the Markdown narrative,
tooling reads the structured records, and everyone can trace a claim to its evidence.

## 1. Founders

**Core question:** *How do AI-first products earn trust, retention and a defensible position?*

Use cases:

- Studying how comparable products handle onboarding, delegation and failure recovery.
- Understanding which trust and control surfaces correlate with sustained use.
- Pressure-testing a product thesis against how incumbents actually behave.

What they should *not* expect: investment advice, valuations, or predictions of commercial
outcomes. The library describes product behaviour and evidence, not market forecasts.

## 2. Product leaders

**Core question:** *What is the right way to evaluate and improve agent UX, memory and control?*

Use cases:

- Adopting the six-dimension method as an internal evaluation rubric.
- Benchmarking a roadmap against documented patterns and anti-patterns.
- Aligning a team on shared vocabulary for agent behaviour and trust.

## 3. Designers

**Core question:** *What interaction patterns make delegation, feedback and repair work?*

Use cases:

- Sourcing concrete, evidenced examples of delegation and correction flows.
- Distinguishing patterns that build trust from those that erode it.
- Grounding design critique in observed behaviour rather than preference.

## 4. Researchers

**Core question:** *Can I reproduce, extend, or refute this analysis?*

Use cases:

- Reusing the evidence hierarchy and confidence rubric in their own work.
- Consuming machine-readable evidence and scoring records for meta-analysis.
- Filing disputes and corrections through a documented process.

## 5. Students

**Core question:** *How do I evaluate an AI-first product rigorously?*

Use cases:

- Learning a transferable method for separating fact, inference and hypothesis.
- Using teardowns as worked examples of sourced, reproducible analysis.
- Practising the method on a product not yet in the library.

## Cross-audience commitments

- **No audience is served by lowering rigour.** Accessibility comes from clear writing and
  layered detail, never from unsupported claims.
- **Every audience can drill down.** A summary claim always links to the evidence and the
  method behind it.
- **Non-technical contributors are first-class.** See
  [`CONTRIBUTING.md`](../../CONTRIBUTING.md) for research-only contribution paths that
  require no code.
